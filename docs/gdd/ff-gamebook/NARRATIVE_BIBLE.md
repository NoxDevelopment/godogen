## PART A — STORY BIBLE (canon)

### A1. Logline

A lone **sin-eater** walks into a plague-shrouded frontier town where the dead are rising to *collect old debts* — and must descend into the drowned Cathedra beneath it to confront the Reckoner who is trying to balance an impossible ledger with the blood of the living, before the whole Verge is "paid in full."

### A2. Themes

Three, stated plainly. Every major beat expresses at least one; the mechanics were chosen to *say* them.

1. **"The ledger is always balanced."** Every gift has a price. This is the spine of the world's cosmology **and** the spine of the systems — LUCK depletes with every Test, Provisions and Gold are finite sinks, and the true path punishes greed. Spending is characterization.
2. **"Debt is a story you tell about the dead."** Grief that refuses to settle becomes horror. The villain is not evil; he is a father who will not close his daughter's account. The player is repeatedly offered the *easy payment* (spend a life, take the shortcut) and the *honest one* (carry the cost yourself).
3. **"Mercy is the only coin that isn't counterfeit."** The one thing the Grey Ledger cannot record is a debt *forgiven* rather than *paid*. The true ending is not a kill — it is a **quittance** (a release). The game quietly teaches that the winning move is to stop playing the ledger's game.

### A3. World & tone

**The realm:** the fading kingdom of **Aldermere**, whose distant capital (Caister-on-Mere) has all but forgotten its northern edge. The game takes place entirely in the **Harrowmarch** — the *Verge of Harrow* — a cold, fog-drowned frontier of peat bog, black pine, and flooded fen, studded with the ruins of an older, richer age.

**The old faith (the horror engine):** centuries ago the Harrowmarch was ruled by the **Order of the Grey Ledger** — the **Ledgerkeepers**, a priesthood who taught that *every life is a debt owed to death, and death keeps books.* They believed a soul could be **assessed, taxed, and its account "balanced"** — that grief was just an *unpaid invoice*, and that a skilled Ledgerkeeper could **defer** a death by transferring the debt to another life. Their great work was the **Cathedra of Accounts**, a subterranean cathedral-counting-house built to "reconcile" the souls of the whole Verge. When Aldermere's old dynasty collapsed, a flood took the lowlands; the Cathedra sank beneath the town of **Harrowfell**, and the Order dwindled to a superstition.

**The rules of the world (what's normal, what's forbidden):**
- Magic is **transactional**, not free. Every working has a *price* paid in something real — years, memory, blood, luck, a name. There are no free miracles. (This is why Potions have doses and codewords have costs.)
- The dead *stay dead* — unless a debt is left open. An **unsettled** death (a broken vow, an unavenged murder, a grief that won't let go) leaves an "account open," and open accounts can be **called in**. This is the in-world explanation for the risen dead.
- To speak a person's **true name** is to hold their account. Names are power and currency both.
- **Forbidden:** the *transfer* of a debt — paying one life with another. The Order did it in secret; it is the original sin of the setting and the engine of the plot.

**The present crisis (what happened before section 1):** Ten years ago, High Ledgerkeeper **Ambrose Vael**'s only daughter, **Isolde**, died of the shivering-fever. Unable to settle his grief, Ambrose broke the Order's deepest law: he descended into the drowned Cathedra and used the old rites to **reopen her account** — pulling her back, but leaving the debt unpaid. Isolde returned *wrong*: neither living nor at rest, her skin scored with the grey ledger-script of the un-reconciled, unable to die and unable to live. To "balance" her, Ambrose — now the thing the survivors call **the Reckoner** — began **collecting**: calling in every open account in the Verge, raising the marked dead as his **Tithe-taken**, and levying a wasting sickness, the **Grey Tithe** (the *Arrears*), on the living to feed the ledger. Harrowfell is the last inhabited town, and it is dying. The player arrives on the last coach north.

**Register / tone of the prose:** grave, plainspoken, cold-weather gothic — Sapkowski's frontier melancholy and M. R. James's dread, not high-fantasy bombast. Money-and-debt vocabulary threads through everything (*owed, arrears, quittance, reckoning, tithe, in the black, settled*). Second person, present-immediate ("The bog exhales. Something in the reeds keeps count of your steps."). Lethal but never nihilist — the world is grieving, not sadistic, and that grief is escapable.

### A4. Characters (principal cast)

The **want / need / voice / arc** blocks below are the *authoritative* character sheets. **The AI DM must be fed the Voice line verbatim** and may never contradict a Want, Need, or continuity fact. "Voice" = 3 adjectives + a speech tic.

---

**1. Mother Grissel Thorne — the plague-nurse (primary ally / quest-giver)**
- **Role:** Hedge-healer and last unofficial authority in Harrowfell; runs the fever-house. Gives the player the quest, the first map, and the game's moral compass.
- **Want (external):** Keep the last few survivors of Harrowfell alive one more night.
- **Need (internal):** To forgive herself — as a girl she was Isolde's nursemaid and gave Ambrose the true-name rite that let him break the law. She *started* this.
- **Voice:** *weary, blunt, tender.* Tic: measures everything in doses and nights ("That'll cost you a night's warmth, no more"). Never lies, but withholds.
- **Arc:** Guilty gatekeeper → confessor (reveals her part if the player earns her trust) → she gives up the one thing that can end it: **Isolde's true name** (codeword `RESTITUTION`), the key to the true ending.
- **DM hooks:** knows Harrowfell's layout, the Reckoner's history, and the "honest path"; will *disapprove* of the player spending lives for shortcuts.

**2. Serjeant Cael Dunmore — the deserter (conditional ally / mirror)**
- **Role:** Last of the Marchwarden militia, barricaded in the flooded moot-hall with three terrified townsfolk. A gate the player can open by trust or force.
- **Want:** Get himself and his charges out of the Verge alive.
- **Need:** Courage — he ran once and the shame is rotting him faster than the Tithe.
- **Voice:** *clipped, ashamed, over-loyal.* Tic: barks orders he no longer has the rank to give ("Hold the line — what's left of it").
- **Arc:** Frightened obstacle → if the player restores his nerve (a specific storylet), he holds the moot-hall stair at the climax so the player can reach the Cathedra; if betrayed or bullied, he breaks and the survivors are lost (a codeword cost the true path can't afford).
- **DM hooks:** the "will you spend others or spare them" test in human form. Combat-capable ally.

**3. Ferrant Coinwright — the last merchant (economy NPC / ambiguous)**
- **Role:** A moneylender-turned-scavenger operating a lantern-lit stall in the drowned market. The game's **shop**: sells Provisions, a torch, rope, a blessed weapon, cures for the Tithe — and *information*, priced in Gold.
- **Want:** Turn the apocalypse into a profit; leave rich.
- **Need:** (deliberately none — he is the setting's argument that *some* debts are just transactions, and that's fine; not everyone needs an arc).
- **Voice:** *oily, precise, unbothered.* Tic: quotes a price for everything, including abstractions ("Courage? Market's thin. I'll do you a discount on rope.").
- **Arc (flat by design):** stable across the game; his stock and prices *react* to the player's Gold and reputation (see BALANCE.md economy). Can be robbed (a codeword cost) or dealt with fairly.
- **DM hooks:** the pressure-valve on the economy; also a lore source (he'll sell the location of the Quittance Seal).

**4. The Reckoner — Ambrose Vael, once High Ledgerkeeper (primary antagonist / final confrontation)**
- **Role:** The mind behind the Grey Tithe. Not a monster-of-the-week — a grieving father who has made himself into an instrument of the ledger. The final section.
- **Want:** **Balance Isolde's account** — collect enough life from the Verge to "pay" for her return and make her whole.
- **Need:** To accept that she is gone and *let the debt go unpaid* — the one act the ledger cannot process.
- **Voice:** *courteous, exhausted, absolutely certain.* Tic: speaks in bookkeeping ("You are, I'm afraid, a receivable"); never raises his voice; apologizes as he kills.
- **Arc:** Fixed in his logic for the whole game; the *player* is his arc — he can be **killed** (pyrrhic ending, ledger uncollected but Tithe continues), **out-argued** with proof of the ledger's lie (a lore path), or **released** — shown that Isolde can be freed, which unmakes his reason to exist (true ending).
- **Cultural-risk note (per benchmark's praise of MGS2's antagonist analysis):** he must read as *tragic*, not as a caricature of grief or faith. The prose never mocks his love; the horror is that love, uncorrected, becomes tyranny. Playtest for "did players feel for him."

**5. Isolde Vael, "the Unpaid" — the resurrected daughter (emotional core / pivotal mid-encounter)**
- **Role:** The heart of the game. Encountered partway through the Cathedra — a girl of twelve, grey ledger-script crawling over her skin, speaking in the voices of the debts she now carries. Neither villain nor victim exactly.
- **Want:** She no longer knows — the ledger has spoken *for* her so long she's forgotten her own voice.
- **Need:** To be *named truly* and released (`RESTITUTION`).
- **Voice:** *layered, childlike-then-ancient, sorrowful.* Tic: switches mid-sentence between a little girl's cadence and the flat recitation of an account ("I want to go home — *balance carried forward, seven souls, interest accruing* — Papa, it's cold").
- **Arc:** Locked door → the true path's key. If the player has `RESTITUTION` and the Quittance Seal, they can free her (true ending). If not, she is a heartbreaking mid-boss (see CONTENT_SAMPLE §Encounter) or a trap.
- **DM hooks:** the "point of no return" emotional gate; her scene is the game's thesis.

**6. Brother Odo Wick — the last acolyte (lore/riddle NPC)**
- **Role:** A half-mad surviving Ledgerkeeper who hid in the Cathedra's archive for a decade, gnawing on old accounts. The game's cryptic-hints dispenser and lore delivery.
- **Want:** To *finish reading the Ledger* — a compulsion, not a plan.
- **Need:** Rest; someone to close the book for him.
- **Voice:** *singsong, allusive, distractible.* Tic: quotes the Order's catechism as riddles ("What is owed at the first gate? *Not gold. Not blood. A name you'd keep.*").
- **Arc:** Gives the player the riddle of the three tithe-gates (a puzzle cluster) and, if treated gently, the location and use of the **Quittance Seal**. Can be killed for a shortcut (a cost).
- **DM hooks:** hint engine; his riddles are the diegetic form of the true-path clues.

**7. The Grey Assessor — recurring servitor / recurring threat**
- **Role:** The Reckoner's toll-wraith: a faceless, robed thing that manifests at thresholds to "assess" the player and demand a tithe (Gold, an item, STAMINA, or a name). Recurs three times, escalating — the game's throughline antagonist before the finale.
- **Voice:** *toneless, patient, plural* ("We find your account… wanting").
- **Function:** the recurring **decision under pressure** — pay the toll (economy sink) or refuse and fight (combat). How the player pays it the first two times gates the third.

**8. Vessel — the ledger-hound (optional companion)**
- **Role:** A grey-marked mastiff, an "account" the Reckoner never collected because a dead shepherd's love kept it open honestly. Can be befriended (Provisions + a kind choice) to become a combat ally and a danger-sense (warns of instant-death sections).
- **Voice:** (non-verbal; described) *loyal, wary, patient.*
- **Function:** the game's proof-of-thesis in miniature — a debt kept open by *love* is not corruption; it's grief done right. Mechanically, an optional ally that meaningfully eases the difficulty for players who chose mercy.

### A5. Factions / groups

| Faction | Goal | Methods | Opposes | Player stance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Order of the Grey Ledger** (Ledgerkeepers, now the Reckoner + Tithe-taken) | "Balance" the Verge; complete Isolde's account | Calling-in of debts, the Grey Tithe sickness, raising the marked dead | The living, the old law they broke | Antagonist; can be *ended* (true path) not just fought |
| **The Tithe-taken** (Ashen Kin) | None of their own — they are conscripted debts | Shamble, collect, drag the living to the Cathedra | The living | Enemies; some can be *released* by name, not killed |
| **The Marchwardens** (Verge militia, collapsed) | Survive; hold Harrowfell | Barricades, a few blades | The Tithe-taken | Potential allies (Cael); fragile |
| **Harrowfell survivors** | Live through the night | Hiding, hedge-healing, trade | Despair | Allies/economy (Grissel, Ferrant); the *stake* — the lives you can spend or save |

### A6. Continuity ledger (the anti-plot-hole file — check before adding any line)

Established facts that **must never be contradicted** by authored sections *or* by the AI DM. Format: fact — **who knows it in-world**.

1. Isolde Vael died of shivering-fever ten years ago and was pulled back by her father — **known fully only to Ambrose and Grissel**; Odo suspects; the town knows only "the Reckoner's ghost-girl."
2. **Grissel gave Ambrose the true-name rite.** She is complicit. — *Only Grissel knows, until she confesses.* Never state it in narration before her confession storylet.
3. The Grey Ledger **cannot record a forgiven debt** — this is the exploitable truth and the true ending's key. — *Odo knows it as catechism; the player must piece it together.* Do not have the Reckoner say it; he *refuses* to know it.
4. **Speaking a true name grants control of that account.** Isolde's true name is the codeword `RESTITUTION`; it is *not* "Isolde" (that is her use-name). — *Only Grissel knows the true name.*
5. The **Quittance Seal** (a Ledgerkeeper's "paid in full" stamp) can *close* an account without payment **only when applied by someone who forgives the debt** — a kill won't trigger it. — *Odo knows where it is; its true use is inferred.*
6. The Reckoner **cannot be permanently stopped by combat alone.** Killing him leaves the ledger open and the Tithe spreads (pyrrhic ending). This is a hard rule — no authored section may grant a "clean" combat win. — *No in-world character knows this; it is a structural truth for the writers.*
7. The Grey Assessor is a *manifestation*, not a person; it recurs and cannot be permanently killed before the finale — "defeating" it disperses it. — *Writers' rule.*
8. Vharn's Flood (the disaster that sank the Cathedra) happened **~120 years ago**; the Order's heyday was **before** that. Isolde's death (10 years ago) is *recent* — Ambrose is an old man who found forbidden old rites, not an ancient lich. Keep the timescale human. — *Common knowledge (the Flood); scholarly (the dates).*
9. **No deity appears or acts.** The "ledger" is a metaphysic, not a god; there is no divine rescue and no clear afterlife shown. Keep it ambiguous and cold.
10. **Gold cannot buy the true ending.** Ferrant sells everything *except* the way out; the resolution is never purchasable. — *Writers' rule; Ferrant would sell it if he could, and says so.*

## PART B — BRANCHING ARCHITECTURE (shape)

### B1. Structure pattern

**Branch-and-bottleneck** (Ashwell), with a **storylet** layer for the town hub and a small **loop-and-grow** for the three tithe-gates. Rationale: a time-cave (pure exploding tree) is unauthorable at ~150–400 sections and impossible to QA; **branch-and-bottleneck scales** — choices diverge meaningfully, then reconverge at load-bearing beats, so the *content between* bottlenecks can be rich without the graph exploding. The town of Harrowfell is a **storylet hub** (content eligible by state, per Emily Short) so the player can explore in any order without combinatorial authoring. This matches the GDD §9a "storylets + weighted decks" design and the ~150–250-section vertical slice.

```
  Act I: THE VERGE (arrival)          Act II: HARROWFELL (hub, storylets)         Act III: THE CATHEDRA (descent)
  ┌───────────┐   ┌───────────┐       ┌─────────────────────────────┐            ┌──────────────┐
  │ §1 arrival│──►│ §First    │──BN1─►│  Town hub (Grissel/Cael/     │──BN2──────►│ Three tithe- │──BN3──►┌──────────┐
  │ (coach)   │   │ Toll      │       │  Ferrant/Odo/Vessel storylets)│            │ gates (loop) │        │ Isolde   │──BN4──►│ Reckoner │──► ENDINGS
  └───────────┘   └───────────┘       └─────────────────────────────┘            └──────────────┘        │ (gate)   │        │ (finale) │
        │                                        │  gather: Seal, RESTITUTION,                             └──────────┘        └──────────┘
        └── instant-death fork (bog)             │  blessed weapon, allies                                     ▲                     │
                                                 └── skip-town shortcut (costs true-path items) ──────────────┘  (locked without RESTITUTION+Seal)
```

BN = **bottleneck** (every player passes through). Between bottlenecks the graph branches widely; at each BN it reconverges.

### B2. The spine (bottlenecks — every player hits these)

1. **BN1 — The First Toll (inciting).** The Grey Assessor stops the player at the ruined toll-bridge into Harrowfell and demands the first tithe. *No one enters the story without being taught the game's central bargain: pay a price or fight.* (See CONTENT_SAMPLE §§1–3.)
2. **BN2 — The Descent Committed (midpoint reversal).** The player enters the Cathedra. This is the point-of-no-return: the town's fate is now *set* by what the player gathered/spent in the hub. The moot-hall either holds (Cael) or falls behind them.
3. **BN3 — The Third Gate (climax gate).** The last tithe-gate demands the price the first two set up; here the game collects on the player's earlier choices (the loop-and-grow pays out).
4. **BN4 — Isolde (ending gate).** The player faces Isolde. **Having `RESTITUTION` + the Quittance Seal here is the sole difference between the true ending and every worse one.** This is the load-bearing gate the whole true path exists to satisfy.
5. **The Reckoner (finale).** Reached by all non-death paths; *which ending fires* is determined by state carried through BN4.

### B3. Branch points (meaningful choices)

| Where | The choice | Diverges on (state set) | Reconverges at | Consequence (what visibly changes) |
|---|---|---|---|---|

| Hub | Earn Grissel's trust (confession storylet) **or** rush past | codeword `RESTITUTION` gained (or not) | BN4 | Without it, the true ending is *unreachable* — the single most important branch |

| Hub | Deal fairly with Ferrant **or** rob him | `ferrantHostile` = bool; Gold | Act III | Robbing gives short-term Gold but closes the blessed-weapon and cure supply |

Nine story variables + the standard sheet. That is deliberately small — the *richness* comes from storylet combinations, not variable count.

### B5. Storylets / gated content (the Harrowfell hub)

The town is authored as **storylets** (Emily Short model): each is a self-contained scene with **prerequisites**, **effects on world-state**, and an **eligibility window**, so the player may visit NPCs in any order without the writers authoring every permutation. This is exactly the GDD §9a "codeword/flag-driven storylets, eligible content chosen by current state" substrate.

| Storylet | Prereq | Effect | Eligible when |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Grissel's Fever-House* | reached hub | opens confession chain | always in Act II |
| *The Confession* | visited Grissel ≥2×, `caelHolds` OR gave a Provision to a survivor (shows mercy) | grants `RESTITUTION` | after trust earned |
| *The Moot-Hall Stair* | reached hub | Cael scene | always |
| *Steady the Serjeant* | passed a Test your Skill (nerve) OR gave Cael the blessed weapon briefly | sets `caelHolds` | after meeting Cael |
| *Ferrant's Stall* | reached hub | shop | always (unless `ferrantHostile`) |
| *Odo in the Archive* | entered Cathedra ante-room | riddle chain → `hasSeal` | Act II late / Act III early |
| *The Grey Hound* | has ≥2 Provisions | `hasVessel` | Act II/III, once |
| *The Assessor Returns* (×2 more) | `tithe_debt` thresholds | escalating toll | between acts |

**Defensive logic (inkle-style):** every storylet checks and re-narrates from *current* state, never assuming an order. Example: the Confession's text branches on whether Cael already fell, so it's coherent whether the player saved the town first or not.

### B6. Endings (list ALL; each names the theme it lands and honors A6)

1. **QUITTANCE (true ending).** Requires `RESTITUTION` + `hasSeal`, used on Isolde at BN4. The player *forgives* the debt: names Isolde truly and seals her account "paid in full" without payment. The Reckoner's reason to exist dissolves; he ages a century in a breath and rests. The Grey Tithe lifts. If `caelHolds`, Harrowfell survives; epilogue reflects who you spared. **Theme:** #3 (mercy is the only real coin). *Honors A6 #3, #5, #6.*
2. **THE HOLLOW VICTORY (pyrrhic / good-bad).** Reckoner killed in combat (needs `blessedWeapon`) *without* freeing Isolde. He dies; the ledger stays open; Isolde wanders on; the Tithe slows but does not stop. "You won the fight. The Verge lost the war." **Theme:** #1 (the ledger is still balanced — against you). *Honors A6 #6.*
3. **THE INHERITED DEBT (dark ending).** Player accepts the Reckoner's offer to *transfer* the debt — spends the survivors (`townLost` forced) to "pay" and walk out alive and wealthy. The Verge is emptied; the player becomes the next Reckoner-in-waiting. **Theme:** #2 (grief/greed uncorrected). Reachable only if the player repeatedly chose to spend others.
4. **CARRIED FORWARD (failure-by-attrition).** Reach the finale without `blessedWeapon` and without the true-path keys → cannot win, cannot flee → the ledger "carries you forward." A soft-lock made *diegetic*: it is an ending (a death), not a bug, and the authoring validator flags any *unintended* version of it (A6 spirit; see B7).
5. **DEATHS (many).** STAMINA 0 or instant-death sections → the Death Screen (GDD §6.12). FF-canonical lethality; each is authored *content* (evocative flavor), not a bare fail-state.

### B7. Coherence & solvability checks

How we prove the graph is sound (mirrors GDD §10 authoring tooling and the §14 "content rots/dead-ends" risk):

- **Link validation:** no dangling "turn to N"; every `target` resolves. (`if-engine` validator, ship-blocking.)
- **Reachability pass:** every section reachable from §1; the **true ending reachable** via at least one fully-resourced path (automated "golden path" trace: §1 → Grissel-trust → Odo/Seal → BN4 → QUITTANCE).
- **Winnability guard:** static check that **no path strips *both* `blessedWeapon` and the true-path keys before BN4** without also offering an exit — i.e., ending #4 only fires where *intended*. Unintended unwinnable states fail the build.
- **Codeword consistency:** `RESTITUTION`, `hasSeal`, `blessedWeapon` each have ≥1 authored source reachable on the golden path; every read has a possible prior write.
- **Fixed-vs-route-dependent fact matrix:** the A6 continuity ledger is encoded as assertions the validator checks against section metadata (e.g., no section may narrate Grissel's guilt before the Confession sets `RESTITUTION`).
- **Dead-end detection:** every non-ending section has ≥1 outbound legal choice from every reachable state.
